Steptorial: Learning about Aphasias

What Is Aphasia?

Aphasia is a disorder that results from damage to portions of the brain that are responsible for language. For most people, these areas are on the left side of the brain. Aphasia usually occurs suddenly, often following a stroke or head injury, but it may also develop slowly, as the result of a brain tumor or a progressive neurological disease. The disorder impairs the expression and understanding of language as well as reading and writing. Aphasia may co-occur with speech disorders, such as dysarthria or apraxia of speech, which also result from brain damage.


Types of Aphasia

There are 3 types of aphasias: Broca’s Aphasia, Wernicke’s Aphasia, and Global Aphasia.


Broca’s Aphasia

In general, Broca’s Aphasia patients understand what other people are saying but have much trouble expressing themselves to other people. Consequently, Broca’s Aphasia is sometimes called “non-fluent aphasia” or “expressive aphasia.”


Symptoms

A Broca’s Aphasia patient suffer from symptoms such as

  • Having difficulty forming sentences
  • Saying something that doesn’t resemble a sentence
  • Being only able to say three or four words at a time
  • Leaving out words like “is” or “the”
  • Having limited vocabulary and trouble finding the words they want to use
  • Understanding speech but having trouble doing so
  • Making mistakes in following directions such as “left” and “right”
  • Using a word that’s close to what you intend, but not the exact word; for example, saying “car” when you mean “truck”


Cause

Broca’s Aphasia is caused by injury to the frontal regions of the left hemisphere, which is the area of the brain that is believed to impact how words are strung together to form complete sentences.


Brief History

In 1861, a French neurosurgeon named Paul Broca examined the brain of a recently deceased patient who had had an unusual disorder. Though the patient had been able to understand spoken language and did not have any motor impairments of the mouth or tongue that might have affected his ability to speak, he could neither speak a complete sentence nor express his thoughts in writing. The only articulate sound he could make was the syllable “tan,” which had come to be used as his name.


Wernicke’s Aphasia

In general, Wernicke’s Aphasia patients, unlike Broca's Aphasia, talk in fluent gibberish and have trouble understanding what other people are saying. Consequently, Wernicke’s Aphasia is sometimes called “fluent aphasia” or “receptive aphasia.”


Symptoms

A Wernicke’s Aphasia patient suffer from symptoms such as

  • Saying words that don’t make sense (i.e., gibberish), e.g., calling a fork a “gleeble”
  • Stringing together a sequence of words that sounds like a sentence but doesn’t make sense


Cause

Wernicke’s Aphasia is caused by lesion to the posterior portion of thee left temporal lobe, which is the area of the brain that is believed to be involved in understanding language.


Brief History

Ten years after Paul Broca’s discovery, a German neurologist named Carl Wernicke discovered another part of the brain that he found was involved in understanding language.


Global Aphasia

In general, Global Aphasia patients have trouble producing and understanding words, and they often can’t read or write. Like Broca's and Wernicke’s Aphasias, Global Aphasia is typically caused by stroke, head injury, or brain tumor.


Some QAs for Further Interests
Do I have aphasia?

You may self-diagnose whether you have any degree of any of Broca’s, Wernicke’s, or Global Aphasia by referencing the lists of symptoms described above. Alternatively, the best way to find out is to go to a doctor. Your doctor will give you a physical and a neurological exam, test your strength, feeling and reflexes, and listen to your heart and the vessels in your neck. In addition, your doctor will likely request an MRI test for you in order to quickly identify the underlying neurological cause for your aphasia, if you do have one.


How do I communicate with someone who has aphasia?

Communicating via the visual and sensory channels is just as important as, if not more important than, communicating via solely the language channel when it comes to establishing effective communications with aphasia patients. For example, you can communicate with such patients using drawings, body languages, or even writings (e.g., flashcards with yes/no questions). But above all, don’t “talk down” to aphasia patients! Give them time to organize their thoughts and speak. Resist the urge to complete their sentences for them. Be patient!


Does aphasia worsen with age?

Yes. Aphasia tends to be progressive, where a patient may first lose the ability to speak and write, and gradually and eventually, lose the ability to understand spokeen and written languages.


Can I learn to speak again if I ever get aphasia?

Yes, but only to an extent. Aphasia patients are like stroke patients, in the sense that both kinds of patients try to express themselves despite their disability. Aphasia has no cure, but patients may still improve over time, especially through speech therapy.
